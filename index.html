What is Linguistic Ethnography?

Linguistic ethnography is a methodological approach that combines ethnographic research with linguistic analysis to understand how language is used in social contexts. As Rampton et al. (2014) explain, it's "ethnography enriched by the study of language" - not simply studying language in isolation, but examining how language practices are embedded in cultural contexts, social relations, and institutional processes.

Defining Linguistic Ethnography

Linguistic ethnography is characterised by:

  • An integrated approach to language and culture
  • Attention to the relationship between language and social structure
  • Focus on the situated use of language in everyday practices
  • Emphasis on the "total linguistic fact" - the inseparability of linguistic form and social context
  • Methodological reflexivity about the researcher's positionality

Practical Example: Linguistic Ethnography in Education

A researcher studying classroom interactions might observe how teachers and students use language to establish authority, negotiate meaning, and construct identities. Rather than just analysing grammar or vocabulary, they would examine how linguistic choices reflect and reinforce power dynamics, cultural expectations, and institutional norms. For example, they might analyse how a teacher's use of specific discourse markers ("well," "you know") signals different types of authority in different contexts.

Linguistic Ethnography in Context: The ANC Card in Uganda

In the Ugandan maternity care context, linguistic ethnography reveals how a simple medical document (the ANC card) functions as more than just a record of health information. It becomes a tool for enforcing compliance with health policies, shaping women's experiences, and mediating relationships between patients and providers. The card's meaning is not fixed but emerges through its use in specific social contexts, demonstrating how language practices are intertwined with institutional processes.

Key Theoretical Concepts in Linguistic Ethnography

Linguistic ethnography draws on a rich theoretical framework from linguistic anthropology and sociolinguistics. These concepts help researchers understand how language functions in social contexts.

The Total Linguistic Fact

As Silverstein (1985) articulated, the "total linguistic fact" refers to the inseparability of linguistic form and social context. Language is never just about words and grammar - it's always embedded in social relations, cultural ideologies, and institutional practices.

Multi-scalar Analysis

Linguistic ethnography requires examining phenomena across multiple scales simultaneously:

  1. Micro-scale: Individual utterances and interactions
  2. Meso-scale: Institutional practices and local communities
  3. Macro-scale: National policies, global discourses, historical processes

As Tusting's (2010) example shows, even a simple nursery nurse's observation form integrates immediate classroom activities with broader social systems including the Early Years Centre's planning procedures, the nurse's career, and government policy.

This illustrates how linguistic ethnography reveals how transnational texts (like the Millennium Development Goals) get operationalised in local contexts, often in ways that reflect power imbalances between Global North and South.

Methods & Data Collection in Linguistic Ethnography

Linguistic ethnography employs diverse methods to capture the complex relationship between language and social context. These methods are designed to capture both the details of linguistic interaction and the broader social context in which they occur.

Linguistic Ethnography Research Cycle

Observation → Data Collection → Analysis → Reflexivity → New Questions

Each stage informs the next in a continuous, iterative process

Primary Data Collection Methods

Participant Observation

The cornerstone method where researchers both observe and participate in community activities over extended periods. Unlike simple observation, participant observation involves active engagement with the social context being studied.

Linguistic Interviews

Structured, semi-structured, and unstructured interviews focused specifically on language use and attitudes. These might include:

  • Stimulated recall interviews: Participants discuss recorded interactions to provide insights about their thinking during those moments
  • Language history interviews: Exploring how individuals' language practices have changed over time
  • Focus groups: Group discussions to explore shared language ideologies and practices

Multimodal Data Collection

Linguistic ethnography recognises that communication involves more than just words:

  • Video recordings of interactions
  • Photographs of physical environments
  • Mapping of spatial relationships
  • Analysis of non-verbal cues (gaze, gesture, posture)

Document Analysis

Analysis of texts that circulate within the social context being studied, including:

  • Policy documents
  • Curriculum materials
  • Official forms and records
  • Media representations
  • Personal diaries and letters

Practical Example: Studying Language in Healthcare Settings

A linguistic ethnographer studying doctor-patient interactions might:

  • Record consultations and take field notes about the physical setting, body language, and interaction patterns
  • Conduct stimulated recall interviews with doctors and patients about specific moments in the consultation
  • Analyse how medical terminology is used differently in different contexts
  • Examine how patient records are created and used across different healthcare settings
  • Study how institutional policies shape communication practices

Global South Context: Texts in Low-Literacy Settings

In the Ugandan maternity care study, linguistic ethnographers had to adapt their methods for a low-literacy context:

  • Focusing on how documents (like ANC cards) function symbolically rather than for their content
  • Observing how texts are handled and used by people who cannot read them
  • Studying oral communication and verbal transmission of information
  • Examining how international texts (like MDGs) get translated into local practices

This demonstrates how linguistic ethnography must be adapted to local contexts and literacy practices.

Linguistic Ethnography in Global South Contexts

Conducting linguistic ethnography in Global South contexts presents unique challenges and opportunities. These contexts often have different literacy practices, communication norms, and power dynamics than those commonly studied in Western contexts.

Key Considerations for Global South Research

Texts and Literacy in Context

In low-literacy settings, texts function differently than in text-saturated Western contexts:

  • Texts may be used symbolically rather than for their content
  • Oral communication often plays a larger role than written communication
  • Transnational texts (from Global North) may dominate local discourse
  • "Absent texts" (those that could exist but don't) can be as significant as existing texts

As Smith (2006) notes: "The magical character of replicable texts... is that they are read, seen, heard, watched in particular local and observable settings while at the same time hooking up an individual's consciousness into relations that are translocal."

Case Study: Maternity Care in Northern Uganda

The ANC card in northern Uganda demonstrates how linguistic ethnography reveals complex relationships between language, power, and health practices:

ANC Card as a "Boss Text"

The ANC card functions as a "boss text" that shapes local practices according to international health priorities. Despite many women being illiterate, the card becomes a symbolic key to delivery care. Women fear being turned away without it, even though they cannot read its contents.

Text-Work-Text Sequences

The ANC card exemplifies a text-work-text sequence:

  1. Text: International health goals (MDGs)
  2. Work: Health administrators interpret and implement these goals
  3. Text: Local policies and practices (ANC card requirements)

This sequence shows how global discourses get operationalised in local contexts, often in ways that reflect power imbalances.

Reflexivity in Global South Research

Researchers must be critically reflexive about their positionality:

  • How does being an outsider (or insider) affect rapport and access?
  • How do power dynamics between researcher and participants shape data collection?
  • How might Western academic frameworks misinterpret local practices?
  • What ethical considerations arise when studying vulnerable populations?

As noted by native scholars conducting research in the Global South: "Our proximity, intellectual care, and own politics are urging us to engage reflexively in conversations and in writing." (Anthropology Encyclopedia)

Practical Example: LC I Letter in Uganda

In the Ugandan maternity care study, women were required to obtain a letter from a Local Council I (LC I) representative if their husbands wouldn't participate in HIV testing. This letter, though not a standardised document, became a powerful text that enforced compliance with health policies.

Linguistic ethnography revealed how this requirement:

  • Reflected gendered power dynamics
  • Was enabled by low literacy and limited access to information
  • Contradicted official policy guidelines
  • Shaped women's healthcare decisions

This example shows how linguistic ethnography can reveal how seemingly minor texts have significant social consequences.

Data Analysis in Linguistic Ethnography

Data analysis in linguistic ethnography requires moving between detailed linguistic analysis and broader social interpretation. It involves examining how language practices are embedded in social contexts and how they shape and are shaped by social relations.

Analysis Frameworks

Analysis Approach Description Example Application Conversation Analysis Micro-analysis of sequential organisation of talk Examining how doctors and patients take turns speaking during consultations Discourse Analysis Analysis of how language constructs social realities How national education policy documents construct certain types of learners and teachers Multimodal Analysis Analysis of multiple modes of communication (language, gesture, image) How a teacher's use of gestures complements verbal instruction in the classroom Indexical Analysis Analysis of how linguistic forms index social identities and relations How a student's use of specific slang terms indexes peer group membership Textual Trajectory Analysis Analysis of how texts move across contexts and transform in meaning How a curriculum document gets interpreted differently in various schools

Analytic Process

Linguistic ethnography analysis involves:

  1. Close reading of data: Paying attention to details of language use, interaction patterns, and contextual features
  2. Connecting micro and macro: Relating specific linguistic instances to broader social structures and processes
  3. Reflexive interpretation: Being aware of how the researcher's positionality shapes interpretation
  4. Contextual grounding: Always locating linguistic phenomena in their specific social contexts
  5. Comparative analysis: Looking for patterns across different instances and contexts

Practical Example: Analyzing the ANC Card in Uganda

When analysing the ANC card data, linguistic ethnographers would:

  • Examine how the card is handled physically (who holds it, where it's kept)
  • Analyse verbal interactions about the card (what people say about it, how they refer to it)
  • Consider how the card functions symbolically despite low literacy
  • Trace its textual trajectory from international health policies to local implementation
  • Examine how it shapes relationships between patients, providers, and community leaders

This analysis reveals how a simple document becomes a powerful tool for enforcing compliance with health policies, even when its content isn't fully understood by users.

Global South Context: Analysing Absent Texts

In low-literacy contexts, linguistic ethnographers must pay attention to what is not present as much as what is present:

  • What texts could exist but don't (e.g., patient-generated complaint letters)
  • How oral communication substitutes for written communication
  • What information is transmitted verbally rather than through documents
  • How power dynamics shape which texts get produced and circulated

As Williams and Rankin (2015) note: "Texts are not necessary to the discovery of ruling relations. [...] while using textual data is one of the important contributions of the IE method, they are not absolutely essential to develop an IE analysis. Other data embedded in people's talk and activities can be used to link people's activities across time and geography."

Key References in Linguistic Ethnography

Rampton, B., Maybin, J., & Roberts, C. (2014). Methodological foundations in linguistic ethnography. King's College London. Available at: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1609406916637088

This foundational paper outlines the theoretical and methodological basis of linguistic ethnography, emphasising the relationship between linguistics and ethnography, the importance of multi-scalar analysis, and the role of reflexivity in research practice.

Blommaert, J. (2005). Discourse: A Critical Introduction. Cambridge University Press.

Blommaert's work is essential for understanding how discourse functions in social contexts, with particular attention to how language practices are embedded in broader social structures and power relations.

Goodwin, C. (2000). Action and Embodiment Within Situated Human Interaction. Journal of Pragmatics, 32, 1489-1522.

Goodwin's work on multimodal analysis is crucial for understanding how communication involves more than just words, including gestures, gaze, and body positioning in interaction.

Smith, D. E. (2006). Institutional Ethnography: A Sociology for People. AltaMira Press.

Smith's work on institutional ethnography provides important insights into how texts function as coordinating mechanisms across different contexts, with particular relevance for understanding how global policies get operationalised locally.

Anthropology Encyclopedia. (2023). Ethnography. Available at: https://www.anthroencyclopedia.com/entry/ethnography

This resource offers critical perspectives on conducting ethnographic research in the Global South, particularly regarding reflexivity, positionality, and ethical considerations for native scholars.

ATLAS.ti. (2025). Ethnographic Research | Definition, Methods & Data. Available at: https://atlasti.com/guides/qualitative-research-guide-part-1/ethnographic-research

Practical guide to data collection and analysis methods in ethnographic research, with specific attention to multimodal data and the integration of different types of evidence.
